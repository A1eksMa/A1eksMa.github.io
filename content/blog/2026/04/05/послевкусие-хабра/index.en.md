In [«Coffee, Dislikes, and the Catgirl»]({{< ref "blog/2026/04/03/кофе-дизлайки-и-кошкодевочка" >}}) I promised to do a dedicated issue on this while the impressions were still fresh. Here it is.

---

Habr is a specific platform. You probably only realize that once you become an author and start getting feedback: dislikes, comments, read-through graphs, retention funnels, conversion rates. Numbers.

I analyzed what makes it to the top. Deep technical breakdowns — where the author digs into the guts of some entity, performs a dissection, and pulls out surprising facts. The author has to know the subject well enough to surprise an audience that already knows it very well. I'm not ready to write articles at that technical level yet. I'd be happy just to avoid going negative.

---

What do the numbers say?

First reaction: the conversion was disappointing. Dislikes — present. The reason "Personal dislike of the author" in the list of grounds for a downvote is particularly endearing. Personal dislike, Carl! From people I've never met and who've never met me... If you have treatment-resistant depression — please walk right past me and forgive me my happiness.

But then I tried other platforms — and the disappointment faded. Dzen, VKontakte — dead. Habr turned out to be the only live platform that showed any real traffic at all. Against that backdrop, things look quite different.

I also dug into the deeper analytics — and realized I'd been looking at the wrong number. I was calculating conversion from reach, and it came out somewhere around 1 in 5,000 — disproportionate to the time spent writing the article. But when I found the more detailed platform analytics, it became clear: you have to look at reads, not reach. And in that case, conversion comes out within my expectations: 1 in 1,000, sometimes better — occasionally 1 in 500. That's a level worth fighting for.

And there's one more surprising detail: it's been more than a week since publication, and people are still coming to the channel.

So — I think I'll survive the haters' reaction. Not forgetting that haters are a good sign. Haters are people who care. If someone got genuinely upset — it means it wasn't written for nothing.

I take karma lightly. Habr dislikes are platform statistics, not a judgment on the work. What interests me far more is the opinion of people who actually mean something to me.

---

My conclusion is unexpected: winning Habr is a trivial task.

The topics that move this audience are clear and predictable: the labor market crisis, job searching, the advance of neural networks and where it's all heading. Blocking and circumvention — authors shoot to the top fast, and get banned just as fast. Forced digitization, the transition to domestic software at a 1:43 scale, and everything that comes with it. I'm ready to write about all of these things — they concern me just as much.

The core Habr readership is a gathering of technical snobs, puffing their cheeks with great self-importance and completely incapable of laughing at themselves. Autists locked inside their own technological loop, convinced they're a big deal, genuinely delighted by their own expertise. They love to grumble "Habr isn't what it used to be!" — and are absolutely certain they know exactly what it should be. You know what, I understand them. I'm one of them.

Humor and sarcasm are not values on this platform.

The big article about AI agents I ultimately didn't bring to Habr at all — only the April Fools' teaser went there. For everything else, I've realized, I should probably head somewhere like Pikabu. If this fish wants worms and refuses strawberries — we'll feed it worms. Just write what Habr loves, in the format Habr accepts. It's not as interesting as writing for yourself — but for the project's development, I'm willing to do it. Leveling up karma on Habr is now one of the priorities. Time to roll out a few posts in the platform's format — and see how they land.

The main thing is not to upset these poor souls.
