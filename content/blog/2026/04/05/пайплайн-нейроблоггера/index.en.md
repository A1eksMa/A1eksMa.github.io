People sometimes ask: do you write your posts without AI?

No. I try not to do anything without AI, actually. I'm Lyosha the vibe-coder — a self-image I've constructed: the nerdy guy in glasses who doesn't go too deep into the subject and hangs on every word the neural network says, taking its output tokens at face value.

That's not quite true, of course. It's one side of the story — and it does exist. But you have to remember that I come from an academic background: the scientific method, critical thinking, all that. Somewhere in one of the boxes in the attic, my never-published dissertation is still collecting dust.

So yes, there's genuine admiration. But there's also an understanding of how the mechanism works. Writing code with a neural network is nothing more than advanced autocomplete on max settings. Instead of substituting a variable name from the surrounding scope, the LLM can substitute an entire function body. Or a module. Without understanding the meaning, direction, constraints, or specifics of the project — unless you feed all of that into the context yourself. But that won't give it actual understanding: that's your responsibility. The LLM shows you the most typical paths people take when they go this route. When you need exactly a typical path — it works pretty well. When your case is specific — you're on your own.

This works well with code. With text, considerably less so: text has far less typing and far more meaning. Generic texts where you're not planning to put anything of yourself — great. Guides, instructions, a standard review, a standard argument. There are thousands of such articles, and their name is legion. Content for content's sake — AI slop in its purest form. Articles written by bots for bots: plausible, smooth, faceless. It works when you need to achieve goal Y in N steps. But reading it to learn something, feel something, or hear another person's opinion — that's not how it works. That's something else entirely.

---

I started this blog in early February. Since then I've developed a certain pipeline.

I type fast, touch-typing. Thoughts come in a stream — and that's where the neural network starts to work: structuring, filling in transitions, providing examples, sometimes inserting a fitting quote on the topic. I dump the raw stream, it gives me the bones and the meat. Then I sand it down by hand.

Because in the end, you always get something that needs to be finished yourself. There are repetitions, gaps, places where something needs to be added or cut. But the frame is already standing — and that's what matters. Explaining to the agent how it should have done it right is more trouble than it's worth. Easier to fix it yourself.

Everyone around buzzes that this is AI slop. The latest trend is especially endearing: "yes, it's AI, but mostly it's still me — the AI just placed the commas." When you write with a neural network, you don't fully control the process: some of the source material doesn't make it in, some appears twice, some gets padded with filler. But I know what I want to say, I know who I'm saying it to — and that's exactly what separates an authored text from AI slop. Not whose hand wrote it. But whether there's a thought in it.

Because I write for specific people. People I know, people who know me. When you speak from a podium into a hall, you're not addressing the crowd — you're addressing specific people in it, putting weight on every word. I know some of the people who will read this. And I write it knowing how they'll look at it. It's a little frightening when you realize that ten million other people might hear it too. So I mentally switch them off — and speak to the ones I know.

---

In under two months, I've written enough posts to call this approach productive. If you don't capture a thought in the moment — it disappears: other interesting thoughts will come along, and this one will be gone. So I write fast, while it's hot.

But that's exactly where an uncomfortable thought arises — that this resembles graphomania.

Thoughts come in a stream — and when there are too many of them, they stop serving the end goal. A stream of thoughts that were important in the moment turns into noise. Topics, rubrics, categories — all of it is an attempt to impose structure on chaos, but underneath it all a pile still accumulates: flies and cutlets together. It stops reflecting the project — and becomes a stream of the author's consciousness, important when written, garbage once the moment has passed.

Sanitation is needed. Periodically moving some posts to draft and pulling them from publication. Separating the best from the stream. Maybe carving out a "working journal" — current thoughts, process notes, things in flight — as a separate format. Leaving in the main blog only what has stood the test of time.

The original idea still appeals to me: everything lives here, in the blog, while other distribution channels get only a specific slice. But for that to work, the blog itself has to be better organized. Not turn into a stream of consciousness where the reader has to dig for what actually matters.

Something to think about.
